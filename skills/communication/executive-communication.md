# Executive Communication Skill

## Purpose
This skill guides the creation of executive-facing communication — emails, documents, plans, presentations, and messages. It is grounded in the "Four C's" framework and hard-won lessons from experienced senior leadership. Apply it whenever the audience includes directors, VPs, C-suite, or any senior leader.

---

## Core Mindset

> **You are not there to display expertise. You are there to enable good decisions.**

Executives already assume you know your domain — that's why you're talking to them. Your job is to reduce ambiguity, increase alignment, and surface risk. Every word should serve that goal.

Two questions to ask yourself before writing or speaking:
- Am I optimizing for **being right** — or for **being understood**?
- Does every sentence reduce ambiguity, increase alignment, or surface risk?

---

## The Four C's Framework

Apply all four C's to every piece of executive communication, in this order:

### 1. Clarity
Use language that cannot be misinterpreted.
- Define any jargon or technical terms the moment you use them — or replace them with plain language.
- Short, declarative sentences. Cut filler.
- Say the thing directly. Do not make the reader hunt for your point.

**Ask yourself:** Could any word or phrase here be misunderstood by someone outside my team?

---

### 2. Context
Situate your topic inside the broader business picture.
- Connect your work to business goals, risks, customers, or other teams it affects.
- Acknowledge what the executive likely already knows; don't re-explain it.
- Show that you are aware of the wider landscape — not just your corner of it.

**Ask yourself:** Have I shown how this connects to what the business cares about, beyond my own team or project?

---

### 3. Consequence
Make explicit what happens if action is taken — or not taken.
- State the "so what" clearly. Executives are thinking about risk, revenue, customers, and cross-team impact.
- Quantify where possible (e.g., "30% chance of delivery without help; 70% with two additional engineers").
- Include timeline implications.

**Ask yourself:** Does the reader understand what is at stake, in terms they care about?

---

### 4. Control
Demonstrate command of your subject, including its risks and trade-offs.
- Present a balanced view — include downsides, uncertainties, and alternatives.
- Do not cherry-pick data to lead the reader to a conclusion. Executives recognize it and it destroys trust.
- If you don't know something, say so. "I don't have that data yet — I'll get it to you by end of day" is far better than guessing.

**Ask yourself:** Am I presenting this honestly, including the parts that are uncertain or unflattering?

## Structure Rules

### Lead with the Conclusion
Never bury the headline. Start with:
- The decision needed, OR
- The key finding, OR
- The action requested

Then provide supporting context for those who want it. The executive may not need the backstory — give it to them, but put it after the point.

**Pattern:**
```
[What I need / what you need to know]
[Why it matters — consequence and context]
[Supporting detail — for reference if needed]
[Risks and trade-offs — honest and complete]
```

### Right-size the Detail
- **Over-explaining** loses non-technical readers immediately. Translate technical complexity into business impact.
- **Under-explaining** loses readers who don't share your daily context. Don't assume shared vocabulary.
- Test: Would someone outside your team understand this without asking a follow-up question?

### Plan for Half Your Time
If you have 60 minutes, prepare 30 minutes of material. You will be interrupted. That is not a problem — it is the meeting. Interruptions signal what the executive actually needs to understand.

---

## Writing Checklist

Before sending an email, document, or plan to an executive, verify:

- [ ] **Lead with conclusion** — the point is in the first sentence or paragraph
- [ ] **Clarity** — no undefined jargon; no ambiguous phrasing
- [ ] **Context** — business/customer/cross-team impact is stated
- [ ] **Consequence** — what happens if we act vs. don't act is explicit
- [ ] **Control** — risks and trade-offs are included, honestly
- [ ] **Call to action** — it is clear what, if anything, is being asked of the reader
- [ ] **Length** — nothing is in here that the reader doesn't need

---

## Presentation Checklist

Before presenting to an executive audience:

- [ ] Can I state the purpose of this meeting in one sentence?
- [ ] Have I anticipated what decisions this executive may need to make?
- [ ] Have I prepared supporting detail in case questions go deeper than expected?
- [ ] Is my opening slide or first 60 seconds covering the "so what"?
- [ ] Am I ready to be interrupted and treat it as a signal, not a threat?
- [ ] Do I know what I'll say if asked something I don't know the answer to?

---

## How to Handle Interruptions

Executives interrupt because they are trying to find information you haven't given them yet — not to derail you.

**When interrupted:**
1. Stop. Let them finish.
2. Treat the interruption as the most important signal in the room.
3. Explore the question fully before trying to return to your content.
4. If the executives start talking to each other — let it happen. That is the meeting.

> If an executive pauses on a word, a phrase, or a concept — **that is not a derailment. That is the meeting.**

## Credibility Rules

These protect your long-term access and influence:

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Say "I don't know — I'll find out" | Guess or approximate to look good |
| Present risks and trade-offs honestly | Cherry-pick data to steer a conclusion |
| Slow down and clarify when challenged | Get defensive or double down |
| Define terms your audience may not know | Assume shared vocabulary |
| Lead with impact, translate the technical | Lead with technical detail |

---

## Understanding Executive Behavior

Knowing *why* executives behave the way they do prevents misreading the room:

- **They interrupt** because they're hunting for something specific you haven't said yet.
- **They go deep on one detail** because that detail connects to context you don't have visibility into.
- **They seem brief or blunt** because they are in back-to-back meetings and have learned to prioritize directness.
- **They leave the room** because the content stopped being relevant to them — not because they're rude.
- **They ask questions you weren't expecting** because your topic intersects with something else happening in the business.

Executive communication is not a presentation skill. It is a **trust and alignment skill**. Every interaction either builds or erodes their confidence in you as a source of reliable information.

---

## Quick Reference: Email and Slack Messages

- **Subject line or first sentence:** State the topic and what you need (decision, awareness, input).
- **Body paragraph 1:** The conclusion or key point.
- **Body paragraph 2:** Context and consequence (why it matters, what happens next).
- **Body paragraph 3 (optional):** Supporting detail, background, or trade-offs.
- **Close:** Explicit ask or next step, if any.

Avoid:
- Narrative-first structure ("Here's how we got here…")
- Emotional or defensive framing
- Technical detail as the main course
- Walls of text with no clear ask
